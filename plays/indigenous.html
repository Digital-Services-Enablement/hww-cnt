Indigenous Right Holders

Understand the relationship between your work and Indigenous Peoples of Canada.

This play will provide you with an overview of the Government of Canada’s relationship with Indigenous Peoples as it relates to modern treaties.

Use this play to...

  • Increase your cultural comprehension of Indigenous Peoples in Canada
  • Gain a basic understanding of treaties
  • Know your working role as it relates to Indigenous Peoples and treaties

Running the Play

This play will provide you with an understanding of treaties, both historical and modern, as well as the Government of Canada’s – and each employee’s – roles and responsibilities when it comes to treaty implementation, consulting Indigenous Peoples, and building and maintaining strong relationships.

By independently reading through Treaties and Modern Treaties you will be given a basic understanding of each section as well as additional tools and resources to further develop your working knowledge; this is a large, complex, and evolving topic of great importance, requiring constant work and commitment.

Treaties

Treaties are agreements made between the Government of Canada, Indigenous groups and often provinces and territories that define ongoing rights and obligations on all sides as it relates to land.

These agreements set out continuing treaty rights and benefits for each group. Treaty rights and Aboriginal rights (commonly referred to as Indigenous rights) are recognized and affirmed in Section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982 and are also a key part of the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples which the Government of Canada has committed to adopt.

Treaties with Indigenous peoples include both:

  • historic treaties with First Nations, and;
  • modern treaties (also called comprehensive land claim agreements) with Indigenous groups.

Modern Treaties

The modern treaty era began in 1973 after the Supreme Court of Canada decision (Calder et al. v. Attorney-General of British Columbia), which recognized Aboriginal rights for the first time. This decision led to the development of the Comprehensive Land Claims Policy and the first modern treaty, the James Bay and Northern Québec Agreement signed in 1975.

Since 1975, Canada has signed 25 additional treaties (called modern treaties or comprehensive land claim agreements) with Indigenous groups in Canada. Some of these treaties include self-government.

These treaties are the basis of the relationship between 97 Indigenous communities (representing about 89,000 Indigenous peoples) and the provincial or territorial and federal governments.

As part of modern treaty negotiations, joint work is done to lay the groundwork for moving forward together after a final agreement has been signed (implementation of the agreement).

Consult the Modern treaties – Comprehensive Land Claims and Self-Government agreements map to learn about the modern treaties in effect to date across Canada and search the Aboriginal and Treaty Rights Information System to find out more about each agreement, including the full text of the agreement and summary information.

The Statement of Principles on the Federal Approach to Modern Treaty Implementation

  1. Modern treaties are a key component of Canadian nation-building
  2. Modern treaties are reconciliation in action
  3. Modern treaty implementation is an ongoing process
  4. The implementation of modern treaties must reflect the agreements entered into
  5. Modern treaties must be implemented in a manner that upholds the honour of the Crown
  6. Aboriginal and treaty rights are constitutionally protected
  7. The implementation of modern treaties supports a set of broad objectives
  8. The implementation of modern treaties is a shared responsibility
  9. The Crown as a whole is accountable for its obligations
  10. All federal departments and agencies will conduct their business in a manner that is consistent with Canada's modern treaty obligations
  11. A whole-of-Government approach to modern treaty implementation is required
  12. Effective intergovernmental relationships are vital to the success of modern treaties

Context

The Cabinet Directive on the Federal Approach to Modern Treaty Implementation provides an operational framework to manage treaty obligations, outlining the responsibilities of federal departments and agencies.

Government of Canada Employees - Treaties and Consultation / Your working relationship with Indigenous Peoples / Duty to Consult

The Government of Canada has a duty to consult with Indigenous Peoples when carrying out many federal activities; the duty to consult is an important part of the federal government's activities, including for regulatory project approvals, licensing and authorization of permits, operational decisions, policy development, negotiations and more.

Government of Canada departments and agencies are responsible for understanding how and when their activities could have an adverse impact on Aboriginal and treaty rights. Crown-Indigenous Relations and Northern Affairs Canada coordinates and advises federal officials on the duty to consult. The attached resource provides information on training, guidelines, protocols, and Memoranda of Understanding.
